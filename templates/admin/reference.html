Diagnosing Common Issues

No Cooling (System Runs, Space Stays Warm)

  1. Verify call: thermostat mode is Cool, setpoint below indoor temp, and Y call is present at air handler or furnace board.
  2. Confirm airflow first: filter condition, blower operation, return and supply restrictions, and visibly iced evaporator.
  3. Check outdoor unit: contactor pulled in, condenser fan running, compressor amp draw compared to nameplate expectations.
  4. Measure split and line temps: suction line should be cool, liquid line warm, and indoor temperature split should generally trend in the expected range once stabilized.
  5. Only then move to refrigerant diagnosis using superheat and subcooling targets from manufacturer data, not guesswork.

Tip: A dirty indoor coil or low airflow can mimic low charge. Always prove airflow before adding refrigerant.

No Heat (Gas Furnace)

  1. Follow ignition sequence in order: inducer, pressure switch, igniter, gas valve, flame prove, blower on-delay.
  2. Check safety circuit continuity: rollout, high-limit, and pressure switch tubing/ports for cracks or blockage.
  3. Confirm gas valve operation only after verifying board output and proper voltage at the valve terminals.
  4. Microamp flame-sense test: weak flame rectification often causes short-cycling lockouts.
  5. Review venting and combustion air if pressure switch faults repeat after component replacement.

Tip: If the same fault returns, step back and verify the condition that triggered it. Do not stack-replace controls.

High Utility / Comfort Complaints

  1. Interview first: when complaint happens, weather conditions, occupancy changes, and thermostat schedule settings.
  2. Check duct leakage, insulation gaps, and obvious bypasses before blaming equipment efficiency.
  3. Verify static pressure and blower speed tap or airflow setting against required CFM for equipment mode.
  4. Compare actual run behavior to load expectation: excessive short cycling often indicates control, oversizing, or airflow issues.
  5. Document baseline readings and post-adjustment readings so customer sees objective improvement.

Tip: Good notes reduce repeat visits and protect younger techs when different people return later.

Part Replacement Process

Before You Remove Anything

  1. Lock out power and verify with your meter. For gas appliances, isolate fuel where required.
  2. Take clear before-photos of wiring, orientation, and mounting hardware.
  3. Confirm replacement part match: rating, voltage, rotation, size, and OEM cross-reference.
  4. Record baseline values (amps, volts, pressures, temperature rise/split) to compare after repair.

Replacement Execution

  1. Label wires as removed, even if colors seem obvious.
  2. Protect nearby components and insulation while removing old hardware.
  3. Install and torque connections securely. Loose electrical and flare connections are callback generators.
  4. Restore routing and strain relief so wires and tubes cannot rub or contact hot surfaces.

Post-Replacement Verification

  1. Cycle system through all operating modes affected by the repair.
  2. Recheck all baseline readings and confirm values moved in the expected direction.
  3. Clear faults and verify no immediate re-trips after a full run cycle.
  4. Explain findings to customer in plain language and document what failed, what was replaced, and final readings.

Tip: Include part number and photos in your notes. It saves time on warranty and future diagnostics.

Common Test Procedures

Electrical Testing Standards

  • Capacitor: verify microfarads under load condition, not only visual condition.
  • Contactor: measure coil voltage and contact voltage drop; pitted contacts can pass voltage but fail under load.
  • Motors/Compressor: compare amp draw to expected load and nameplate, then investigate cause before replacing.
  • Low-voltage controls: confirm call in and call out at each control stage to find where command is lost.

Airflow and Temperature Testing

  • Total external static pressure: compare to blower table and required CFM target.
  • Temperature rise (heating): verify within manufacturer listed rise range, then adjust airflow if out of range.
  • Delta T (cooling): use stabilized readings after airflow confirmation for meaningful interpretation.
  • Filter, wheel, and coil inspection: contamination drives poor airflow and false refrigerant conclusions.

Refrigeration and Combustion Process Checks

  • Refrigerant: use superheat/subcooling with outdoor and indoor conditions documented on the ticket.
  • Leak search: pressurize, isolate, then verify with electronic detector and bubble confirmation where needed.
  • Combustion (gas heat): inspect heat exchanger area, flame pattern, and vent draft behavior before final sign-off.
  • Safety controls: test high limits and pressure-related safeties in a controlled way where procedures permit.

Tip: When readings conflict, trust process over assumptions. Re-verify instruments, then re-run the sequence.
